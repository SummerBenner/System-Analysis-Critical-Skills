DriverPass Project Reflection
The DriverPass project focused on designing a complete system that would help students better prepare for their DMV driving tests. The client, DriverPass, wanted a system that combined online learning, practice exams, and a scheduling platform for on‑the‑road driving lessons. Their goal was to reduce the high failure rate of driving tests by offering structured training tools, real‑time scheduling, and up‑to‑date practice materials. To meet these needs, I designed both a Business Requirements Document (Project One) and a System Design Document (Project Two), which together outline how the system should function and how it should be built.

One area I believe I did particularly well was translating the interview transcript into clear, actionable requirements. The client described many operational challenges—such as scheduling conflicts, outdated test materials, and the need for role‑based access—and I was able to convert those into structured functional and nonfunctional requirements. I also feel confident about the clarity of my UML diagrams in the System Design Document. They accurately represent the system’s behavior and structure, and they align with the client’s expectations.

If I were to revise one part of my work, I would expand the narrative depth in the Business Requirements Document. While the requirements were accurate, I could strengthen the document by adding more explanation about why each requirement matters and how it supports the client’s goals. Adding more context from the transcript and elaborating on the reasoning behind each requirement would make the document even more complete and professional.

To interpret the user’s needs, I focused closely on the interview transcript and identified the pain points described by each stakeholder. For example, the secretary needed a simple way to schedule lessons for customers, the IT officer needed tools to manage accounts, and Liam needed access to reports and business data. I implemented these needs into the system design by creating role‑specific features, defining clear system components, and ensuring that the architecture supported scalability and security. Considering user needs is essential in system design because it ensures that the final product is functional, intuitive, and aligned with real‑world workflows. A system that ignores user needs will ultimately fail, regardless of how well it is engineered.

My approach to designing software involves breaking the problem into smaller components and using modeling techniques to understand the system from multiple perspectives. Process modeling helps me visualize workflows and identify decision points, while object modeling helps me define the system’s structure and relationships. In future projects, I plan to continue using UML diagrams, iterative refinement, and user‑centered design strategies. These techniques help ensure that the system is both technically sound and aligned with stakeholder expectations.
